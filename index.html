\ What actually happened\

Six phases.
One direction.

\ \
\ \
\
Phase 1
\ [YEAR]\

The Business Was Working.

\

24 years spent building a business \'97 a reputation. A restaurant in your own hometown. Real people. Community events. A place friends and family gathered, celebrated, commiserated. Then it was gone.

\
\ \
\
Phase 2
\ [COVID YEARS]\

Everything Stopped. We Adapted.

\

Mandated closures. Capacity limits. Disappearing revenue. Skyrocketing prices. Fixed costs \'97 remained fixed. We pivoted, got innovative, applied for relief, worked harder than ever. Survival mode was indefinite. The cost was immediate. The pressure was enormous. Stress \'97 the new norm.

\
\ \
\
Phase 3
\ [YEAR]\

The Landlord Moved First.

\

We signed a short-term amended rent schedule to help get through the pandemic. Then the landlord sued us for late rent. Then proceeded to threaten eviction \'97 repeatedly \'97 during the last 2 years of our lease. He's an attorney, and this was a calculated strategy. Legal on paper. Predatory in practice.

\
\ \
\
Phase 4
\ [YEAR]\

The Simultaneous Sledgehammer.

\

We received an IRS notice. A massive debt we were previously unaware of \'97 TFRP, for those of you who know what that is. The IRS levied our bank accounts in January 2022. Again in May. Again in July. Thus began the true beginning of the end.

\

IRS levy. Returned checks. Bank fees. IRS levy. Returned payroll checks. More bank fees. Rent had to be paid. IRS levy. We couldn't make payroll \'97 we had to call the IRS, call the bank, submit a form just so we could operate.

\

We submitted an OIC. They denied it, kept the check \'97 then the bank fired us. And rent was due.

\

Three institutions. Maximum pressure. All at once. All during COVID. This was always only going to end one way.

\
\ \
\
Phase 5
\ [YEAR]\

The Doors Closed.

\

Not dramatically. Not loudly. Just \'97 done. We reminisced. We laughed. We talked about which regulars we'd miss the most \'97 and which ones we probably wouldn't \'97 as we packed up the last 24 years of our lives. That last glimpse of the empty building that had been our second home will always be bittersweet.

\
\ \
\
Phase 6
\ Now\

The Aftermath.

\

We closed our doors two years ago. We sit here today personally carrying a massive IRS debt \'97 still with no answers, no resolution, no acknowledgment, no escape.

\

COVID wasn't just a "thing" for businesses to survive. For far too many small business owners, COVID was personal. COVID was transformative.

\

What do you do when you wake up the day after you close the restaurant you opened and ran every day for 24 years \'97 with no restaurant to go to? For me, what came next is what this page is actually about.

\ Pattern recognition\

This is what systemic
failure looks like.

\ \
\
\
01
\

The language changes.

\

Lease terms get reinterpreted. Verbal agreements vanish. What was promised in conversation is suddenly "not in the contract."

\
\
\
02
\

The timeline compression.

\

Deadlines move faster than resolution can travel. You're given 30 days to solve what took years to build.

\
\
\
03
\

The simultaneous pressure.

\

One institution is manageable. Two is a grind. Three at once \'97 landlord, bank, federal agency \'97 is a coordinated weight no small operator is built to absorb.

\
\
\
04
\

The help that arrives late.

\

SBA programs. State relief. Federal aid. Some real, most inaccessible. Designed for businesses with accounting departments. We were a small team.

\
\
\
05
\

The isolation.

\

The stigma of a closed business runs deep in a culture that treats failure as character defect. Most people going through this say nothing publicly. This one will.

\
\ \
\
\
\ \ \
\
\ What it did to us\

The part nobody
talks about.

\

Sleep changed. The kind of rest that restores you becomes rare when your nervous system is running threat assessment around the clock.

\

Identity took a hit. When you've built something with your hands for 24 years, losing it isn't just financial. It's a question about who you are when the thing you built is gone.

\

The grief was quiet. No one holds a funeral for a closed business. There's no ritual. You just stop going to a place that used to be yours.

\

And then \'97 slowly \'97 something else started.
Not optimism. More like: refusal.

\

The refusal to let this be the whole story. The refusal to disappear into the wreckage quietly. That refusal needed a shape. It became a Heart.

\
\
\ \ \
\
\ \uc0\u9829 The object\

Something had to
come from it.

\ \

The first Heart wasn't made to sell. It was made to cope.

\

I started working with polymer clay during one of the hardest stretches of my life — the kind where getting out of bed felt like a victory and anxiety lived in my chest like a weight I couldn't shake. I wasn't trying to make anything. I just needed my hands to do something while my brain tried to make sense of everything falling apart.

\

And what they kept forming — gripping that clay like it was the only solid thing left — were Hearts.

\

Simple. Imperfect. But something about them felt… right.

\

I'd hold one while I worked through a hard conversation. I'd keep another on my desk where I could see it. And without meaning to, I started associating each Heart with what I was carrying — this one held my grief, that one reminded me to breathe, another was my "you've got this" on the days I didn't believe it.

\

They became my external hard drive for emotions I couldn't hold on my own in that particular moment.

\

Staring at this pile of perfectly imperfect Hearts, I realized they weren't just helping me survive. They were giving me something tangible to anchor to when everything felt untethered. A visual Reminder. A touchstone. A permission slip to feel what I was feeling — without drowning in it.

\

That's when I knew I wanted to share them.

\

Because if a little clay Heart could hold space for my hardest days, maybe it could do the same for yours.

\ \
